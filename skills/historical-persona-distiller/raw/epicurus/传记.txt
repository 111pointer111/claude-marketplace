# 伊壁鸠鲁传记 - 百度百科

## 基本信息
- 生卒年：公元前341年－前270年
- 地区：古希腊
- 流派：伊壁鸠鲁学派（享乐主义）
- 地点：萨摩斯岛（Samos）出生，后在雅典建立「花园」学派

## 生平
伊壁鸠鲁（Epicurus），古希腊哲学家，伊壁鸠鲁学派创始人。他的哲学强调 pleasure（快乐/幸福）是最高的善，教导人们追求适度的享受和心灵的宁静。

### 出生与早年
伊壁鸠鲁出生于公元前341年，在雅典的一个殖民城市萨摩斯。他的父亲是一位学校教师。伊壁鸠鲁在年轻时就开始研究哲学，曾师从德谟克利特学派的哲学家。

### 创立学派
公元前306年，伊壁鸠鲁在雅典购买了一处房产，里面有一个花园。他在那里建立了自己的学校——「花园」（The Garden）。这个学校与柏拉图的学园（Academy）和亚里士多德的吕克昂（Lyceum）不同，向所有人开放，包括女性、奴隶和穷人。

### 哲学教学
伊壁鸠鲁的哲学核心是：
- 快乐是最高的善
- 痛苦是最大的恶
- 人生的目的是追求快乐和避免痛苦
- 通过理性和哲学来达到心灵的宁静（ataraxia）

### 晚年
伊壁鸠鲁一生写了大约300篇著作，但大多已失传。他的晚年是在病痛中度过的，但他仍然继续教学和写作。

### 去世
伊壁鸠鲁于公元前270年去世，享年约71岁。

## 主要思想

### 物理学
伊壁鸠鲁继承并发展了德谟克利特的原子论，认为：
- 宇宙由原子（atomos）和虚空（void）组成
- 原子在虚空中运动
- 一切现象都可以用原子运动来解释
- 神存在但不会干预人间事务

### 伦理学
伊壁鸠鲁的伦理学核心是享乐主义，但他强调的是「理性的享乐」：
- 不是所有的快乐都值得追求
- 某些痛苦可能带来更大的快乐
- 心灵的宁静比感官的享受更重要

### 逻辑学
伊壁鸠鲁发展了一套「准则学」（Canoniology），作为判断真理的标准。

## 历史影响
伊壁鸠鲁的思想对后世产生了深远影响：
- 影响了罗马时期的卢克莱修（Lucretius）
- 影响了文艺复兴时期的人文主义者
- 影响了近代的功利主义哲学

# 古希腊哲学家伊壁鸠鲁的生平与思想简介

伊壁鸠鲁（Epicurus，约公元前341—前270年）是古希腊哲学家、伦理学家，伊壁鸠鲁学派的创始人。他的思想主要集中在伦理学领域，其核心观点是「快乐是最高的善」。

伊壁鸠鲁出生于萨摩斯岛（Samos），父亲是一位教师。他很早就开始学习哲学，曾广泛游历，在公元前306年左右来到雅典，并买下了一处带有花园的房产，建立了著名的「花园学派」。

### 主要思想

**1. 原子论物理学**
伊壁鸠鲁继承并改造了德谟克利特的原子论，提出原子不仅会直线下落，还会产生「偏移」（clinamen），这种偏移是自由意志的物理基础。他的物理学主张用自然原因解释一切现象，反对宗教迷信和宿命论。

**2. 快乐主义伦理学**
伊壁鸠鲁所说的「快乐」主要指「身体的无痛苦」和「心灵的宁静」（ataraxia）。他反对极端的肉体享乐，认为真正的幸福在于理性的、有节制的生活。他将欲望分为三类：自然且必要的（如饮食）、自然但不必要的（如奢华饮食）、不自然也不必要的（如虚荣和权力欲）。

**3. 神学观**
伊壁鸠鲁认为神虽然存在，但它们幸福且不朽，不会关心或干预人间事务。人们对神的恐惧和对死亡的恐惧是人生烦恼的主要来源，因此理解自然是解除恐惧的关键。

### 主要著作
伊壁鸠鲁的著作大多已失传，保存下来的主要有：
- 《自然论》（On Nature）
- 《准则》（Canon）
- 《论关系》（On Relations）
- 以及一些书信和语录集

著名的《致美诺西乌斯书》（Letter to Menoeceus）是他伦理思想的精华。

# Epicurus - World History Encyclopedia

Epicurus (c. 341-270 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher and the founder of Epicureanism. Born on the island of Samos, he eventually settled in Athens where he established a school known as "The Garden." His philosophy centered on achieving ataraxia (peace of mind) through the pursuit of pleasure and avoidance of pain.

## Life

Epicurus was born around 341 BCE on the Greek island of Samos. His father was a schoolmaster. From an early age, Epicurus showed an interest in philosophy, and he studied under Demetrius of Lampsacus and possibly the Atomist Nausiphanes. By the time he was thirty-two, Epicurus had already begun teaching his own philosophical doctrines.

Around 306 BCE, Epicurus moved to Athens, where he would establish his famous school. Unlike other philosophical schools of the time, The Garden was notably inclusive, welcoming women, slaves, and the poor. This was revolutionary for ancient Athens. He attracted a following of disciples, who would continue to spread his teachings after his death.

Epicurus was incredibly prolific, reportedly writing over 300 works on topics ranging from physics to ethics. However, the vast majority of these writings have been lost. What we know about his philosophy comes primarily from secondary sources like Diogenes Laertius and from fragments preserved in the works of later authors like Lucretius and Cicero.

## Philosophy

Epicurus's philosophy can be divided into three main areas: canonic, physics, and ethics.

### Canonic
The Epicurean canon was the system Epicurus used to determine truth. It consisted of three criteria: sensations, pre-conceptions (prolepses), and feelings of pleasure and pain. According to Epicurus, these criteria were infallible and served as the foundation for all knowledge.

### Physics
Epicurus's physics was heavily influenced by the work of Democritus and Leucippus. He believed that the universe was composed of atoms moving through a void. However, he introduced one crucial modification to the atomic theory: the concept of the swerve (parenklisis). This was a random deviation in the motion of atoms that allowed for indeterminism in the universe and provided a physical basis for free will.

Epicurus's physics also included a materialist theory of the soul. He believed that the soul was composed of fine atoms dispersed throughout the body and that when the body died, the soul also dissolved. This meant that there was no afterlife to fear, which was a comforting thought for many of his followers.

### Ethics
The ultimate goal of Epicurean philosophy was ataraxia (peace of mind or tranquility) and aponia (freedom from bodily pain). According to Epicurus, the highest good was pleasure, and pain was the highest evil. However, his concept of pleasure was not the hedonistic indulgence often associated with his name. Rather, Epicurus championed a life of moderate enjoyment, free from unnecessary pain and anxiety.

For Epicurus, not all pleasures were desirable. Some pleasures would lead to greater pain in the future, and therefore should be avoided. The wise person would carefully consider the consequences of their actions and choose those pleasures that would lead to the greatest long-term happiness.

The Epicurean paradox, also known as the problem of evil, was Epicurus's way of addressing the issue of suffering in a world supposedly governed by gods. He argued that if the gods were both powerful and benevolent, they would want to prevent evil in the world. If they were unable to do so, they would not be all-powerful. And if they had no desire to do so, they would not be benevolent. Since evil clearly exists in the world, either the gods do not care about human suffering, or they are not all-powerful.

## Legacy

After his death, Epicurus's school continued to operate in Athens for several centuries. His philosophy was later adopted and modified by the Roman poet Lucretius in his work "De rerum natura" (On the Nature of Things).

The influence of Epicureanism continued into the Renaissance and was rediscovered by scholars studying classical texts. Today, the term "epicurean" is often used to describe a person who is devoted to refined sensory enjoyment, particularly in food and drink. However, this modern usage is a far cry from the philosophy of Epicurus himself, who advocated for a simple, moderate lifestyle free from anxiety and unnecessary suffering.

# Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy - Epicurus

## Life and Works

Epicurus (341-271/0 BCE) was an ancient Greek philosopher who founded one of the most influential schools of antiquity. He was born on the island of Samos in 341 BCE and died in Athens in 271/0 BCE. He established his philosophical school, known as "The Garden," in Athens around 306 BCE. The school attracted a diverse community of philosophers, including women and slaves, which was unusual for the time.

## Philosophy Overview

### Core Tenets

**Pleasure as the Highest Good**
Epicurus taught that pleasure is the highest good and the ultimate aim of all human action. However, he distinguished between different types of pleasure:
- **Moving pleasures**: experienced in the process of satisfying a desire
- **Static pleasures**: the feeling of satisfaction and contentment when desire is fulfilled

He argued that static pleasures (katastematic pleasures) were superior because they don't create new desires that could lead to dissatisfaction.

### The Goal of Life: Ataraxia and Aponia
Epicurus's ethics aimed at two goals:
1. **Ataraxia** (peace of mind/tranquility)
2. **Aponia** (freedom from bodily pain)

He believed that these could be achieved through a life of philosophical inquiry and moderate pleasure-seeking.

### The Three Types of Desires
Epicurus classified desires into three categories:
1. **Natural and necessary** (e.g., thirst, hunger) - should be satisfied
2. **Natural but not necessary** (e.g., elaborate food) - can be satisfied in moderation
3. **Vain and empty** (e.g., fame, power, immortality) - should be avoided

### Death and the Fear of Death
One of Epicurus's most famous teachings was his argument that death is nothing to us. He reasoned that:
- When we exist, death has not yet come
- When death comes, we no longer exist
- Therefore, death is not an evil to be feared

This argument was designed to free people from the fear of death and allow them to focus on living well in the present.

### The Gods and Religion
Epicurus believed that the gods existed but that they were perfect and self-sufficient beings who had no need to intervene in human affairs. He argued that the traditional view of gods as concerned with human matters was a source of unnecessary anxiety and superstition.

### Physics and Atomism
Like Democritus before him, Epicurus believed that the universe was made up of atoms moving through empty space. However, he introduced the concept of the "swerve" (clinamen) to allow for indeterminism and free will in the physical world.

### Political Philosophy
Epicurus advocated for living in quiet seclusion rather than participating in public life. He believed that political involvement would bring unnecessary trouble and anxiety. His ideal was a small community of friends living simply and peacefully together.

## Key Texts

The main surviving texts from Epicurus include:
- **Letter to Menoeceus**: Summarizes his ethical teachings
- **Letter to Herodotus**: On physics and atomism
- **Letter to Pythocles**: On celestial phenomena
- **Principal Doctrines (Kuriai Doxai)**: A collection of 40 short statements summarizing his philosophy

# Principal Doctrines (Epicurus) - via Diogenes Laertius

## Key Principles

1. "The just is happiest of all, and the unjust, whoever he may be, is most wretched."
2. "Nature's wealth at once is limited and easy to procure; but the wealth of idle fancies is without limit and hard to reach."
3. "The magnitude of pleasure reaches its limit in the removal of all pain."
4. "Pleasure is not increased by augmenting pleasure, but only by varying it."
5. "The wise man's power is sufficient to make him self-sufficing."
6. "The object of life is not to live as the many live, but to live happily."
7. "No pleasure is a bad thing in itself."
8. "We must consider that some desires are natural and necessary, some are natural and not necessary, and some are neither natural nor necessary."
9. "The soul is not immune to the troubles of the body."
10. "We must not violate the laws of justice, but we may wisely enjoy the natural gifts of fortune."
11. "Justice is not something that exists in itself, but arises from mutual cooperation."
12. "The wise man will not expose himself to danger, nor trust his life to luck."
13. "Friendship is the greatest of all goods that wisdom provides for the blessed life."
14. "The just man enjoys the greatest peace of mind, while the unjust is always in turmoil."
15. "We should not fear the gods, nor death, for the wise man knows that the gods are not to be feared and that death is nothing to us."

# Additional Notes on Epicurus's Philosophy

## What Epicurus Actually Taught

**Ataraxia (Peace of Mind)**
The ultimate goal of Epicurean philosophy was ataraxia - a state of tranquility and freedom from anxiety. This was achieved by:
- Understanding the true nature of the universe
- Distinguishing between necessary and unnecessary desires
- Cultivating friendships
- Living a simple, self-sufficient life

**The Role of Friendship**
Epicurus placed great emphasis on friendship as essential to the happy life. He believed that:
- Friends provide mutual support and security
- The greatest pleasure comes from sharing simple things with friends
- A circle of loyal friends is the best protection against life's uncertainties

**Simple Living**
Unlike the popular stereotype, Epicurus advocated for simple living:
- Plain food and water bring as much pleasure as elaborate meals
- True wealth is being able to be content with little
- Luxurious things are only enjoyable when earned through simple habits

**Logic and Epistemology**
Epicurus's epistemology was based on sensations and feelings as the criteria of truth. He believed that:
- Sensations are always true and never deceiving
- Our pre-conceptions (prolepses) are formed from accumulated sensations
- Feelings of pleasure and pain tell us what to pursue and avoid
