## 一、传记引语（斯宾诺莎传，俞磊，home.yulei.org）

> "很好，这样我就不必强迫我去做我本意所不愿意做的任何事情了。"
> ——斯宾诺莎面对被革除教门时的回应（1656年）

> "当我受到经验的教训之后,才深悟得日常生活中习见的一切东西，都是虚幻的、无谓的。……那些在生活中最常见，并且由人们的行为所表明，被当作是最高幸福的东西，归纳起来，大约不外三项：财富、荣誉和感官快乐。……我彻底下决心，放弃迷乱人心的财富、荣誉、肉体快乐这三种东西，则我所放弃的必定是真正的恶，而我所得到的必定是真正的善。"
> ——《知性改进论》（1661年）

> "要达到斯宾诺莎的哲学成就是不容易的，要达到斯宾诺莎的人格是不可能的。"
> ——黑格尔

> "所有我们现代的哲学家，都是透过斯宾诺莎磨制的眼镜来看世界的。"
> ——海涅

## 二、《伦理学》引语（Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy 引用）

> "By substance I understand what is in itself and is conceived through itself."
> "By attribute I understand what the intellect perceives of a substance, as constituting its essence."
> "By God I understand a being absolutely infinite, i.e., a substance consisting of an infinity of attributes, of which each one expresses an eternal and infinite essence."

> "Whatever is, is in God, and nothing can be or be conceived without God." (Ip15)

> "all things have necessarily flowed, or always followed, by the same necessity and in the same way as from the nature of a triangle it follows, from eternity and to eternity, that its three angles are equal to two right angles" (Ip17s1)

> "In nature there is nothing contingent, but all things have been determined from the necessity of the divine nature to exist and produce an effect in a certain way." (Ip29)

> "Things could have been produced by God in no other way, and in no other order than they have been produced." (Ip33)

> "a mode of extension and the idea of that mode are one and the same thing, but expressed in two ways" (IIp7s)

> "the order and connection of ideas is the same as the order and connection of things" (IIp7)

> "A true idea means nothing other than knowing a thing perfectly, or in the best way" (IIp43s)

> "It is of the nature of Reason to regard things as necessary, not as contingent." (IIp44)

> "In the Mind there is no absolute, or free, will, but the Mind is determined to will this or that by a cause that is also determined by another, and this again by another, and so to infinity" (IIp48)

> "We are driven about in many ways by external causes, and … like waves on the sea, driven by contrary winds, we toss about, not knowing our outcome and fate" (IIIp59s)

> "Nature has no end set before it … All things proceed by a certain eternal necessity of nature." (I, Appendix)

> "One who seeks the true causes of miracles, and is eager, like an educated man, to understand natural things, not to wonder at them, like a fool, is generally considered and denounced as an impious heretic by those whom the people honor as interpreters of nature and the Gods. For they know that if ignorance is taken away, then foolish wonder, the only means they have of arguing and defending their authority is also taken away." (I, Appendix)

> "That eternal and infinite being we call God, or Nature, acts from the same necessity from which he exists" (Part IV, Preface, Deus sive Natura)
