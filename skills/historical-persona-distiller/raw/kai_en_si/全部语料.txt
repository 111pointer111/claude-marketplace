## 传记核心 (Britannica)

John Maynard Keynes (born June 5, 1883, Cambridge, Cambridgeshire, England—died April 21, 1946, Firle, Sussex) was an English economist, journalist, and financier best known for his economic theories (Keynesian economics) on the causes of prolonged unemployment. His most important work, The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money (1935–36), advocated a remedy for economic recession based on a government-sponsored policy of full employment.

Keynes was born into a moderately prosperous family. His father, John Neville Keynes, was an economist and later an academic administrator at King's College, Cambridge. His mother was one of the first female graduates of the same university.

At Cambridge he was influenced by economist Alfred Marshall, who prompted Keynes to shift his academic interests from mathematics and the classics to politics and economics. Cambridge also introduced Keynes to the Bloomsbury group.

After earning a B.A. in 1905 and an M.A. in 1909, Keynes became a civil servant, taking a job with the India Office in Whitehall. His experience there formed the basis of his first major work, Indian Currency and Finance (1913).

With the onset of World War I, Keynes returned to government employment, this time in the Treasury. Accompanying Prime Minister David Lloyd George to France as an economic adviser at Versailles, Keynes was troubled by the political chicanery and burdensome policies to be imposed upon Germany. He resigned his post, depressed, by the impending "devastation of Europe" (letter to his father).

In two summer months he composed The Economic Consequences of the Peace (1919). The book contained blistering sketches of Woodrow Wilson, Georges Clemenceau, and Lloyd George.

In 1925 he opposed Britain's return to the gold standard at the prewar dollar-to-pound ratio of $4.86. He wrote The Economic Consequences of Mr. Churchill and continued to argue against the gold standard until Britain finally abandoned it in 1931.

Keynes's magnum opus, The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money, was published in 1936. It challenged the earlier neoclassical economic paradigm, which had held that provided it was unfettered by government interference, the market would naturally establish full employment equilibrium.

Keynes argued that demand is what creates supply and not the other way around. Without government intervention to increase expenditure, an economy can remain trapped in a low-employment equilibrium.

In 1937 he suffered a severe heart attack. In 1944 he played a prominent role at the Bretton Woods Conference. He died the following year.

## 生平节点 (Wikipedia)

### 早年 1883-1904
Born in Cambridge to John Neville Keynes (economist) and Florence Ada Keynes (social reformer). Educated at Eton, then King's College Cambridge (mathematics). Influenced by Alfred Marshall who persuaded him to shift to economics. Active in Cambridge Apostles society.

### 职业生涯早期 1906-1914
1906: Civil Service clerk at India Office. 1908: Resigned to return to Cambridge, working on probability theory. 1909: Became fellow of King's College. 1911: Made editor of The Economic Journal. 1913: Published "Indian Currency and Finance."

### 一战时期 1914-1918
1915: Took position at Treasury. 1917: Appointed Companion of the Order of the Bath for wartime work. 1919: Appointed financial representative at Versailles — resigned in protest.

### 《和平的经济后果》1919
Published The Economic Consequences of the Peace in December 1919.

### 1920年代 1920-1929
1921: Published "A Treatise on Probability." 1923: Published "A Tract on Monetary Reform" — argued against gold standard. 1925: Married Lydia Lopokova. 1926: Published "The End of Laissez-faire." 1928: Published "The Means to Prosperity."

### 大萧条与《通论》1929-1936
1930: Published "A Treatise on Money." 1933: Published "The Means to Prosperity" — early mention of multiplier effect. 1934: Met with Franklin D. Roosevelt. 1936: Published "The General Theory."

### 晚年 1937-1946
1937: Severe heart attack. 1940: Published "How to Pay for the War." 1942: Created Baron Keynes. 1944: Led British delegation at Bretton Woods. 1945: Negotiated loan from United States. 1946: Died April 21.
