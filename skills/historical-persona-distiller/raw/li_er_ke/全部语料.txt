# 里尔克（Rainer Maria Rilke）语料来源汇总

## 来源1：Britannica — Rilke Biography
URL: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rainer-Maria-Rilke

Rainer Maria Rilke (born December 4, 1875, Prague, Bohemia, [Austria-Hungary] [now in Czech Republic]—died December 29, 1926, Valmont, Switzerland) was an Austro-German poet who became internationally famous with his lyric poems and such works as Duino Elegies (1923) and Sonnets to Orpheus (1923). Since his death in 1926, he has grown in reputation as one of the greatest German-language poets, widely admired for the posthumously published works Letters to a Young Poet (1929) and Visions of Christ (1959).

## Rilke 早年与教育
Rilke was the only son of an unhappy marriage. His father, Josef Rilke, a civil servant, was frustrated in his career; his mother, Sophie Rilke (née Entz), the daughter of an upper-middle-class merchant and imperial councillor, felt that she had married beneath herself. She left her husband in 1884 and moved to Vienna so that she could be close to the imperial court.

Rilke's education was ill planned and fragmentary. It had been decided that he was to become an officer to assure him the social standing barred to his father. Consequently, after some years at a rather select school run by the Piarist religious brothers of Prague, he was enrolled in the military lower realschule of Sankt Pölten (Austria) and four years later entered the military upper realschule at Mährisch-Weisskirchen (Bohemia). These two schools were completely at variance with the needs of this highly sensitive boy, and he was finally forced to leave the school prematurely because of poor health. In later life he called these years a time of merciless affliction, a "primer of horror."

After another futile year spent at the Academy of Business Administration at Linz (1891–92), Rilke, with the energetic help of a paternal uncle, was able to straighten out his misguided educational career. In the summer of 1895 he completed the course of studies at the German gymnasium of the Prague suburb of Neustadt.

By the time he left school, Rilke had already published a volume of poetry (Leben und Lieder, 1894; "Life and Songs"), and he had no doubt that he would pursue a literary career. Matriculating at Prague's Charles University in 1895, he enrolled in courses in German literature and art history and, to appease his family, read one semester of law. But he could not become really involved in his studies, and so in 1896 he left school and went to Munich, a city whose artistic and cosmopolitan atmosphere held a strong appeal. Thus began Rilke's mature life—the restless travels of a man driven by his inner needs and of the artist who managed to persuade others of the validity of his vision.

## 与 Lou Andreas-Salomé 的关系及俄罗斯之旅
In May 1897 Rilke met the writer Lou Andreas-Salomé, who shortly became his lover. Salomé, nearly 15 years older than Rilke, was from St. Petersburg, the daughter of a Russian general and a German and Danish mother. In her youth she had been wooed by, and had refused, the philosopher Friedrich Nietzsche, and 10 years before her meeting with Rilke she had married a German professor. Rilke's affair with Salomé was a turning point in his life. More than a mere romantic partner, she was a surrogate mother, the leading influence in his éducation sentimentale, and, above all, the person who introduced Russia to him. Moreover, she persuaded him to change his first name from René to Rainer, believing it to sound more "masculine."

Russia was a milestone in Rilke's life. It was the first and most incisive of a series of "elective homelands," leaving a deeper mark than any of his subsequent discoveries with the possible exception of Paris. He and Salomé visited Russia first in the spring of 1899 and then in the summer of 1900. There he found an external reality that he saw as the ideal symbol of his feelings, his inner reality. Russia for him was imbued with an amorphous, elemental, almost religiously moving quality—a harmonious, powerful constellation of "God," "human community," and "nature"—the distillation of the "cosmic" spirit of being.

## 《祈祷书》（The Book of Hours）
Russia evoked in Rilke a poetic response that he later said marked the true beginning of his serious work: a long three-part cycle of poems written between 1899 and 1903, Das Stunden-Buch (1905; The Book of Hours). In this work the poetic "I" presents himself to the reader in the guise of a young monk who circles his god with swarms of prayers, a god conceived as the incarnation of "life," as the numinous quality of the inner-worldly diversity of "things."

"God speaks to each of us as he makes us,
then walks with us silently out of the night. These are the words we dimly hear:
You, sent out beyond your recall,
go to the limits of your longing.
Embody me."

## 沃普斯韦德与婚姻
Soon after his second trip to Russia, Rilke joined the artists' colony of Worpswede, near Bremen, where he hoped to settle down among congenial artists experimenting with developing a new lifestyle. In April 1901 he married Clara Westhoff, a young sculptor from Bremen who had studied with Auguste Rodin. The couple set up housekeeping in a farm cottage in nearby Westerwede. There Rilke worked on the second part of Das Stunden-Buch and also wrote a book about the Worpswede colony (published in 1903). In December 1901 Westhoff gave birth to a daughter, and soon afterward the two decided on a friendly separation so as to be free to pursue their separate careers.

## 巴黎与罗丹
Rilke was commissioned by a German publisher to write a book about Rodin and went to Paris, where the sculptor lived, in 1902. For the next 12 years Paris was the geographic center of Rilke's life. He frequently left the city for visits to other cities and countries...

Rilke's Paris was not the belle époque capital steeped in luxury and eroticism; it was a city of abysmal, dehumanizing misery, of the faceless and the dispossessed, and of the aged, sick, and dying. It was the capital of fear, poverty, and death. His preoccupation with these phenomena combined with a second one: his growing awareness of new approaches to art and creativity, an awareness gained through his association with Rodin. Their friendship lasted until the spring of 1906. Rodin taught him his personal art ethic of unremitting work, which stood in sharp contrast to the traditional idea of artistic inspiration. Rodin's method was one of dedication to detail and nuance and of unswerving search for "form" in the sense of concentration and objectivization.

## 新诗派（New Poems）
During those Paris years Rilke developed a new style of lyric poetry, the so-called Ding-Gedicht ("object poem"), which attempts to capture the plastic essence of a physical object. Some of the most successful of these poems are imaginative verbal translations of certain works of the visual arts.

These Neue Gedichte (1907–08; New Poems) represented a departure from traditional German lyric poetry. Rilke forced his language to such extremes of subtlety and refinement that it may be characterized as a distinct art among other arts and a language distinct from existing languages.

In a letter to Lou Andreas-Salomé written in July 1903, he had defined his method with this formulation: "making objects out of fear."

## 《马尔特·劳里茨·布里格笔记》
Die Aufzeichnungen des Malte Laurids Brigge (1910; The Notebook of Malte Laurids Brigge), on which he began work in Rome in 1904, is a prose counterpart to Neue Gedichte. However, that which hovers in the background in the poems, behind the perfection of style, is in the foreground of the prose work: the subjective, personal problems of the lonely occupant of a Paris hotel room, the "fear" that is the inspiration for the creation of "the objects."

In this work are found all of Rilke's major themes: love, death, the fears of childhood, the idolization of woman, and, finally, the matter of "God," which is treated simply as a "tendency of the heart." The work must be seen as the description of the disintegration of a soul—but a disintegration not devoid of a dialectic mental reservation: "Only a step," writes Malte, "and my deepest misery could turn into bliss."

## 写作瓶颈与军事服役
The price Rilke paid for these masterpieces was a writing block and depression so severe that it led him to toy with the idea of giving up writing. Aside from a short poetry cycle, Das Marienleben (1913; The Life of the Virgin Mary), he did not publish anything for 13 years.

At the outbreak of World War I Rilke was in Munich, where he decided to remain, spending most of the war there. In December 1915 he was called up for military service with the Austrian army at Vienna, but by June 1916 he had returned to civilian life.

## 杜伊诺哀歌
The Duino Elegies, series of 10 poems by Rainer Maria Rilke, published in German as Duineser Elegien in 1923. Acknowledged as Rilke's finest achievement and one of the century's poetic masterpieces, the Duino Elegies is praised for its supple language, its experimentation with metre and rhyme, and its profound meditation on human existence. Scholars note that the poems are more elegiac in mood than in form.

The cycle was conceived as a whole, although the poems were composed over a period of 10 years. Rilke wrote the first two elegies, and began the third, while visiting the castle of Duino on the Adriatic in 1912; he finished the third in Paris in 1913 and completed the fourth and began the fifth in Munich in 1915. Emotionally paralyzed by World War I and its aftermath, Rilke wrote little more until February 1922, when in a burst of nearly manic productivity he revised the fifth poem, completed the remaining five of the cycle, and wrote the 55 poems that comprise the Sonnets to Orpheus, all in a span of three weeks.

## 致奥尔弗斯的十四行诗
Sonnets to Orpheus, series of 55 poems in two linked cycles by Rainer Maria Rilke, published in German in 1923 as Die Sonette an Orpheus. The Sonnets to Orpheus brought Rilke international fame. The Sonnets to Orpheus are concerned with the relationship of art and poetry to life. In them Rilke sought to show poetry's power to transmute problems of existence and to justify reality.

## 来源2：poets.org — Academy of American Poets Biography
URL: https://poets.org/poet/rainer-maria-rilke

Rainer Maria Rilke was born on December 4, 1875, in Prague. His parents placed him in military school with the desire that he become an officer—a position Rilke was not inclined to hold. With the help of his uncle, who realized that Rilke was a highly gifted child, Rilke left the military academy and entered a German preparatory school. By the time he enrolled at Charles University in Prague in 1895, he knew that he would pursue a literary career: he had already published his first volume of poetry, Leben und Lieder, the previous year.

In 1897, Rilke went to Russia, a trip that would prove to be a milestone in Rilke's life, and which marked the beginning of his early, serious works. While there, the young poet met Leo Tolstoy, whose influence is seen in Das Buch vom lieben Gott und anderes [Stories of God], and Leonid Pasternak, the nine-year-old Boris's father.

At Worpswede, where Rilke lived for a time, he met and married Clara Westhoff, who had been a pupil of Auguste Rodin. In 1902, he became the friend and, for a time, the secretary of Rodin. It was during his twelve-year Paris residence that Rilke enjoyed his greatest poetic activity. His first great work, Das Stunden Buch [The Book of Hours], appeared in 1905, followed in 1907 by Neue Gedichte [New Poems] and Die Aufzeichnungen des Malte Laurids Brigge [The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge].

When World War I broke out, Rilke was obliged to leave France. During the war, he moved back to Munich. In 1919, Rilke went to Switzerland, where he spent the last years of his life. It was here that he wrote his last two works, the Duino Elegies (1923) and the Sonnets to Orpheus (1923).

Rilke died of leukemia on December 29, 1926, in Valmont, Switzerland.

### 人物经历

1875年12月4日，勒内·马利亚·里尔克生于布拉格。1884年双亲离异后，跟随母亲生活。1886年9月1日作为助学金领取者进圣珀尔腾初级军校。这段生活他后来说成是沉重的灾难。写第一首诗。

1890年初级军校毕业后，转入梅里希·魏斯基尔欣高级军校。1891年因病退离高级军校。开始在林茨的商业学院就读，规定三年课程，亦半途而废。1892年秋季开始私自准备参加中学毕业考试。1894年报刊上发表许多单篇作品后，独自出版第一本诗集《生活与歌曲》。

1895年在布拉格参加中学毕业考试（"成绩优异"），自冬季学期起在布拉格大学学习：艺术史，文学史，哲学。1896年夏季学期转入布拉格大学法学-政治学系。迁居慕尼黑，选修两学期艺术史（文艺复兴）美学，达尔文学说。

1897年，诗人初访威尼斯。1898年，旅游阿尔果，佛罗伦萨。遇斯特凡·格奥尔格和亨利希·福格勒。5月在维亚雷焦，6月在柏林。

1899年，偕安德烈亚斯夫妇初访俄罗斯（华沙，莫斯科，访问托尔斯泰，彼得堡）。1900年从5月到8月偕卢·安德烈亚斯-莎乐美二访俄罗斯。8月27日前往沃尔普斯威德，结识女画家保拉·摩德尔松-贝克尔和女雕塑家克拉拉·韦斯特霍夫（后为里尔克夫人）。1901年4月28日与克拉拉·韦斯特霍夫结婚，1901年12月12日生独生女露特。

1902年8月28日到1903年6月底初次旅居巴黎。9月1日访罗丹，《图像集》出版，十一月写出《新诗集》第一首《豹》。

1905年到1906年期间里尔克一度担任罗丹的私人秘书。因为在巴黎的这段时间里而诞生的作品有《新诗集》（Neue Gedichte）（1907年）以及《续新诗》（Der neuen Gedichte anderer Teil）（1908年）、《安魂曲》（Requiem）（1909年）和在1904年开始撰写的小说《布拉格手记》（1910年）。

1910年5月31日《马尔特·劳里茨·布里格笔记》出版。1910年11月19日至1911年3月29日旅游北非（阿尔及尔、突尼斯，埃及——卢克索，凯尔奈克）。

1912年从1911年10月22日到1912年5月9日，在杜伊诺堡。撰写第一批《哀歌》（第十首开头，第一、二首片断）和《马利亚生平》。

1914年8月在格廷根，适逢大战爆发，丧失留在巴黎的全部所有。1915年11月作第四首《杜伊诺哀歌》。12月13日在维也纳，住塔克西斯侯爵夫人家中。1916年在维也纳服兵役，自元月起在军事档案馆任文书。6月9日复员。

1919年6月11日里尔克从慕尼黑来到了瑞士苏黎世。1921年里尔克发现了缪佐特（Muzot）小城堡。1922年5月里尔克的朋友莱茵哈特将这座城堡买下赠与诗人。

1922年二月在短短的几个星期内里尔克灵感迸发，完成了长达十年的《杜伊诺哀歌》的创作，并且在这段时间里尔克还完成了另一部巨著《致奥尔弗斯的十四行诗》（Sonette an Orpheus）。

1925年1月8日至8月18日最后一次旅居巴黎，与翻译者摩里斯·贝茨谈话。与巴拉迪内·克洛索夫斯卡同居。

1926年12月29日因白血病逝世。

### 个人生活
赖内·玛利亚·里尔克于1875年出生于布拉格的一个小市民家庭，父亲是一个仕途不甚如意、退而求其次的铁路小吏，终身都在为自己郁郁不得志而怨天尤人。母亲的性格恰恰相反，张扬且虚荣。自小在这样的环境中生活，里尔克的内心不得不变得纤细且谨小慎微。因为姐姐刚出生不久就夭折了，里尔克一来到这个世界上，就被母亲当作女孩子来养，给他留着长长的卷发，穿女孩的衣服，给他买的都是布娃娃玩具。

### 创作特点
**孤独思考**：在里尔克看来，诗人需要"将物从常规习俗的沉重而无意义的关系中提升出来，恢复到其本质的巨大关联之中"，这就是"创造物"，经由创造性行为，物变形为"艺术-物"，一种更加内在、确定、完美的物。这种物因其摆脱了时间的逝性而获得了一种永恒的平静。

**宗教思考**：里尔克的大部分作品与"上帝"、宗教有着不解之缘。里尔克将基督教的上帝和众神分离开来。他的上帝是"内心的上帝"，而非教会意义上的基督教上帝。

### 人物影响
赖内·玛利亚·里尔克与叶芝、艾略特被誉为欧洲现代最伟大的三位诗人。里尔克的存在主义诗思更是深深地影响到后来的存在主义大师海德格尔与萨特等人，可以说是存在主义的一大诗性源头。

### 人物评价
"里尔克是'中世纪以来操德语的民族拥有的伟大的诗人'。'第一次使德语诗歌臻于完美'。"（奥地利作家罗伯特·慕齐尔评）

"poems are not…simply emotions…they are experiences. For the sake of a single poem, you must see many cities, many people and things…and know the gestures which small flowers make when they open in the morning…" —The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge
