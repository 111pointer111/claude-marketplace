Ernest Hemingway (born July 21, 1899, Cicero [now in Oak Park], [Illinois](https://www.britannica.com/place/Illinois-state), U.S.—died July 2, 1961, Ketchum, Idaho) was an American novelist and [short-story](https://www.britannica.com/art/short-story) writer who was awarded the [Nobel Prize for Literature](https://www.britannica.com/art/Winners-of-the-Nobel-Prize-for-Literature-1856938) in 1954. He was noted both for the intense masculinity of his writing and for his adventurous and widely publicized life. His [succinct](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/succinct) and lucid prose style exerted a powerful influence on American and British [fiction](https://www.britannica.com/art/fiction-literature) in the 20th century.

## Young Hemingway

 The first son of Clarence Edmonds Hemingway, a doctor, and Grace Hall Hemingway, Ernest Miller Hemingway was born in a suburb of [Chicago](https://www.britannica.com/place/Chicago). He was educated in the public schools and began to write in [high school](https://www.britannica.com/topic/high-school), where he was active and outstanding, but the parts of his boyhood that mattered most were summers spent with his family on Walloon Lake in upper [Michigan](https://www.britannica.com/place/Michigan). On graduation from high school in 1917, impatient for a less-sheltered [environment](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/environment), he did not enter college but went to [Kansas City](https://www.britannica.com/place/Kansas-City-Missouri), where he was employed as a reporter for the Star.

 Hemingway was repeatedly rejected for [military service](https://www.britannica.com/topic/armed-force) because of a defective eye, but he managed to enter [World War I](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-I) as an ambulance driver for the [American Red Cross](https://www.britannica.com/topic/American-Red-Cross). On July 8, 1918, not yet 19 years old, he was injured on the Austro-Italian front at Fossalta di Piave. Decorated for heroism and hospitalized in Milan, he fell in love with a [Red Cross](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Red-Cross-and-Red-Crescent) nurse, Agnes von Kurowsky, who declined to marry him. These were experiences he was never to forget.

 After [recuperating](https://www.britannica.com/dictionary/recuperating) at home, Hemingway renewed his efforts at writing, for a while worked at odd jobs in Chicago, and sailed for [France](https://www.britannica.com/place/France) as a foreign correspondent for the Toronto Star. Advised and encouraged by other American writers in [Paris](https://www.britannica.com/place/Paris)—[F. Scott Fitzgerald](https://www.britannica.com/biography/F-Scott-Fitzgerald), [Gertrude Stein](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gertrude-Stein), [Ezra Pound](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Ezra-Pound)—he began to see his nonjournalistic work appear in print there, and in 1925 his first important book, a collection of stories called [In Our Time](https://www.britannica.com/topic/In-Our-Time) was published in New York City; it was originally released in Paris in 1924.

## The making of a writer

 In 1926 he published [The Sun Also Rises](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Sun-Also-Rises), a [novel](https://www.britannica.com/art/novel) with which he scored his first solid success. A pessimistic but sparkling book, it deals with a group of aimless expatriates in France and [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/place/Spain)—members of the postwar [Lost Generation](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Lost-Generation), a phrase that Hemingway [scorned](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/scorned) while making it famous. This work also introduced him to the limelight, which he both craved and resented for the rest of his life. Hemingway's The Torrents of Spring, a [parody](https://www.britannica.com/art/parody-literature) of the American writer [Sherwood Anderson](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sherwood-Anderson)'s book Dark Laughter, also appeared in 1926.

## A seminal work

 At least in the public view, however, the novel [A Farewell to Arms](https://www.britannica.com/topic/A-Farewell-to-Arms-novel) (1929) overshadowed such works. Reaching back to his experience as a young soldier in [Italy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Italy), Hemingway developed a grim but lyrical novel of [great power](https://www.britannica.com/topic/great-power), fusing love story with war story. While serving with the Italian ambulance service during World War I, the American lieutenant Frederic Henry falls in love with the English nurse Catherine Barkley, who tends him during his recuperation after being [wounded](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/wounded). She becomes pregnant by him, but he must return to his post. Henry deserts during the Italians' disastrous retreat after the [Battle of Caporetto](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-Caporetto), and the reunited couple flee Italy by crossing the border into [Switzerland](https://www.britannica.com/place/Switzerland). There, however, Catherine and her baby die during childbirth, and Henry is left desolate at the loss of the great love of his life.

## The Spanish influence

 Hemingway's love of Spain and his passion for [bullfighting](https://www.britannica.com/sports/bullfighting) resulted in [Death in the Afternoon](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Death-in-the-Afternoon) (1932), a learned study of a spectacle he saw more as tragic ceremony than as sport. Similarly, a safari he took in 1933–34 in the big-game region of [Tanganyika](https://www.britannica.com/place/Tanganyika) resulted in Green Hills of Africa (1935), an account of big-game hunting. Mostly for the fishing, he purchased a house in [Key West](https://www.britannica.com/place/Key-West), Florida, and bought his own fishing boat. A minor novel of 1937 called [To Have and Have Not](https://www.britannica.com/topic/To-Have-and-Have-Not) is about a [Caribbean](https://www.britannica.com/place/Caribbean-Sea) desperado and is set against a background of lower-class violence and upper-class decadence in Key West during the [Great Depression](https://www.britannica.com/event/Great-Depression).

 By now [Spain](https://www.britannica.com/event/Spanish-Civil-War) was in the midst of civil war. Still deeply attached to that country, Hemingway made four trips there, once more a correspondent. He raised money for the Republicans in their struggle against the Nationalists under General [Francisco Franco](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Francisco-Franco), and he wrote a play called The Fifth Column (1938), which is set in besieged [Madrid](https://www.britannica.com/place/Madrid). As in many of his books, the [protagonist](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/protagonist) of the play is based on the author. Following his last visit to the Spanish war, he purchased Finca Vigía ("Lookout Farm"), an unpretentious estate outside [Havana](https://www.britannica.com/place/Havana), Cuba, and went to cover another war—the Japanese invasion of [China](https://www.britannica.com/place/China).

 The harvest of Hemingway's considerable experience of Spain in war and peace was the novel [For Whom the Bell Tolls](https://www.britannica.com/topic/For-Whom-the-Bell-Tolls-novel-by-Hemingway) (1940), a substantial and impressive work that some critics consider his finest novel, in preference to A Farewell to Arms. It was also the most successful of all his books as measured in sales. Set during the Spanish Civil War, it tells of Robert Jordan, an American volunteer who is sent to join a [guerrilla](https://www.britannica.com/topic/guerrilla) band behind the Nationalist lines in the Guadarrama Mountains. Most of the novel concerns Jordan's relations with the varied personalities of the band, including the girl Maria, with whom he falls in love. Through [dialogue](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/dialogue), flashbacks, and stories, Hemingway offers telling and vivid profiles of the Spanish character and unsparingly depicts the cruelty and inhumanity stirred up by the civil war. Jordan's mission is to blow up a strategic bridge near [Segovia](https://www.britannica.com/place/Segovia-Spain) in order to aid a coming Republican attack, which he realizes is doomed to fail. In an atmosphere of impending disaster, he blows up the bridge but is wounded and makes his retreating comrades leave him behind, where he prepares a last-minute resistance to his Nationalist pursuers.

## Hemingway's relationship to war

 All of his life Hemingway was fascinated by war—in A Farewell to Arms he focused on its pointlessness, in For Whom the Bell Tolls on the comradeship it creates—and, as [World War II](https://www.britannica.com/event/World-War-II) progressed, he made his way to [London](https://www.britannica.com/place/London) as a journalist. He flew several missions with the [Royal Air Force](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Royal-Air-Force) and crossed the [English Channel](https://www.britannica.com/place/English-Channel) with American troops on D-Day (June 6, 1944). Attaching himself to the 22nd Regiment of the 4th Infantry Division, he saw a good deal of action in [Normandy](https://www.britannica.com/place/Normandy) and in the [Battle of the Bulge](https://www.britannica.com/event/Battle-of-the-Bulge). He also participated in the liberation of Paris, and, although ostensibly a journalist, he impressed professional soldiers not only as a man of [courage](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/courage) in battle but also as a real expert in military matters, guerrilla activities, and intelligence collection.

 Following the war in [Europe](https://www.britannica.com/topic/history-of-Europe), Hemingway returned to his home in [Cuba](https://www.britannica.com/place/Cuba) and began to work seriously again. He also traveled widely, and, on a trip to [Africa](https://www.britannica.com/place/Africa), he was injured in a plane crash. Soon after (in 1953), he received the [Pulitzer Prize](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Pulitzer-Prize) in fiction for [The Old Man and the Sea](https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Old-Man-and-the-Sea-novel-by-Hemingway) (1952), a short heroic novel about an old Cuban fisherman who, after an extended struggle, hooks and boats a giant [marlin](https://www.britannica.com/animal/marlin) only to have it eaten by voracious [sharks](https://www.britannica.com/animal/shark) during the long voyage home. This book, which played a role in gaining for Hemingway the Nobel Prize for Literature in 1954, was as enthusiastically praised as his previous novel, Across the River and into the Trees (1950), the story of a professional army officer who dies while on leave in [Venice](https://www.britannica.com/place/Venice), had been damned.

 By 1960 Hemingway had left Cuba and settled in Ketchum, [Idaho](https://www.britannica.com/place/Idaho). (He expressed his belief in what he called the "historical necessity" of the [Cuban Revolution](https://www.britannica.com/event/Cuban-Revolution); his attitude toward its leader, [Fidel Castro](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Fidel-Castro), who had taken power in 1959, varied.) He tried to lead his life and do his work as before. For a while he succeeded, but, anxiety-ridden and depressed, he was twice hospitalized at the [Mayo Clinic](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mayo-Clinic) in Rochester, [Minnesota](https://www.britannica.com/place/Minnesota), where he received electroshock [treatments](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/treatments). Two days after his return to the house in Ketchum, he took his life with a shotgun. Hemingway had been married four times: to Hadley Richardson in 1921 (divorced 1927), Pauline Pfeiffer in 1927 (divorced 1940), [Martha Gellhorn](https://www.britannica.com/biography/Martha-Gellhorn) in 1940 (divorced 1945), and Mary Welsh in 1946. He had fathered three sons: John Hadley Nicanor ("Bumby"), with Hadley, born in 1923; Patrick, with Pauline, in 1928; and Gregory, also with Pauline, in 1931.

## The Hemingway legacy

 Hemingway left behind a substantial amount of manuscript, some of which has been published. [A Moveable Feast](https://www.britannica.com/topic/A-Moveable-Feast), an entertaining [memoir](https://www.britannica.com/topic/memoir-historical-genre) of his years in Paris (1921–26) before he was famous, was issued in 1964. Islands in the Stream, three closely related [novellas](https://www.britannica.com/art/novella) growing directly out of his peacetime memories of the Caribbean island of [Bimini](https://www.britannica.com/place/Bimini-Islands), of Havana during World War II, and of searching for U-boats off Cuba, appeared in 1970.

 Hemingway's characters plainly embody his own values and view of life. The main characters of The Sun Also Rises, A Farewell to Arms, and For Whom the Bell Tolls are young men whose strength and self-confidence nevertheless coexist with a sensitivity that leaves them deeply scarred by their wartime experiences. War was for Hemingway a potent symbol of the world, which he viewed as complex, filled with [moral](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/moral) [ambiguities](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ambiguities), and offering almost unavoidable pain, hurt, and destruction. To survive in such a world, and perhaps emerge victorious, one must conduct oneself with honor, courage, endurance, and dignity, a set of principles known as "the Hemingway code." To behave well in the lonely, losing battle with life is to show "grace under pressure" and [constitutes](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/constitutes) in itself a kind of victory, a theme clearly established in The Old Man and the Sea.

 Hemingway's prose style was probably the most widely imitated of any in the 20th century. He wished to strip his own use of language of inessentials, ridding it of all traces of verbosity, embellishment, and sentimentality. In striving to be as objective and honest as possible, Hemingway hit upon the device of describing a series of actions by using short, simple sentences from which all comment or emotional [rhetoric](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/rhetoric) has been eliminated. These sentences are composed largely of nouns and verbs, have few adjectives and adverbs, and rely on repetition and rhythm for much of their effect. The resulting terse, concentrated prose is concrete and unemotional yet is often resonant and capable of conveying great [irony](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/irony) through understatement. Hemingway's use of dialogue was similarly fresh, simple, and natural-sounding. The influence of this style was felt worldwide wherever novels were written, particularly from the 1930s through the '50s.

 A consummately contradictory man, Hemingway achieved a fame surpassed by few, if any, American authors of the 20th century. The virile nature of his writing, which attempted to re-create the exact physical sensations he experienced in wartime, big-game hunting, and bullfighting, in fact masked an [aesthetic](https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/aesthetic) sensibility of great delicacy. He was a celebrity long before he reached [middle age](https://www.britannica.com/science/middle-age), but his popularity continues to be validated by serious critical opinion.
