# Alan Turing — Life Stages

## Stage 1: Early Life & Schooling (1912–1930)
**Age:** 0–18 | **Years:** 1912–1930

### Key Events
- Born June 23, 1912, in Maida Vale, London; father Julius Turing was in Indian Civil Service
- Attended St Michael's primary school; headmistress noted: "had clever boys and hardworking boys, but Alan is a genius"
- Educated at Hazelhurst Preparatory School (1922–1926), then Sherborne School (1926–1931)
- At Sherborne, formed deep friendship with Christopher Morcom
- Christopher Morcom died February 13, 1930, from bovine tuberculosis — Turing coped by intensifying work in science and mathematics
- Wrote letters to Mrs. Morcom expressing grief and his resolve to honor Morcom's memory through work

### Language Style at Stage
- Introspective, emotionally intense, philosophical for his age
- Letters to Mrs. Morcom show a mix of grief, loyalty, and intellectual aspiration

### Representative Works
- Early self-study of Einstein's work (age 16) — deduced Einstein's questioning of Newton's laws
- Solved advanced problems in 1927 without having studied elementary calculus

## Stage 2: Cambridge, Princeton & the Turing Machine (1931–1939)
**Age:** 19–27 | **Years:** 1931–1939

### Key Events
- Entered King's College, Cambridge (1931) on scholarship; studied mathematics
- Graduated with first-class honours (1934); dissertation proved a version of the central limit theorem
- Elected Fellow of King's College (1935) for research in probability theory
- Published seminal paper "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem" (1936)
  - Introduced the Turing machine concept; proved the Entscheidungsproblem undecidable
  - Paper described as "easily the most influential math paper in history"
- Ph.D. from Princeton University (1938) under Alonzo Church; dissertation introduced ordinal logic and oracles
- Attended Wittgenstein's lectures on foundations of mathematics (1939); defended formalism against Wittgenstein's conventionalism
- Joined Government Code and Cypher School (GC&CS), part-time from 1938

### Language Style at Stage
- Precise, formal, mathematical — his papers use careful definitions and absolute precision
- Defended formalism in philosophical arguments; willing to argue with Wittgenstein

### Representative Works
- "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem" (1936)
- "Systems of Logic Based on Ordinals" (PhD dissertation, 1938)

## Stage 3: Bletchley Park & Wartime Cryptanalysis (1939–1945)
**Age:** 27–33 | **Years:** 1939–1945

### Key Events
- Reported to Bletchley Park (September 4, 1939), the day after Britain declared war on Germany
- Led Hut 8, the section responsible for German naval cryptanalysis
- Designed the functional specification of the Bombe (improvement on Polish Bomba method)
- Developed Banburismus (statistical procedure for optimizing code-breaking)
- Devised Turingery (method for working out cam settings of Lorenz SZ 40/42 cipher machine)
- By early 1942, Bletchley Park was decoding ~39,000 intercepted messages/month (rising to 84,000/month)
- Developed portable secure voice scrambler (Delilah) at Hanslope Park
- Made OBE (Officer of the Most Excellent Order of the British Empire) for code-breaking work
- Known at Bletchley as "Prof"; treatise on Enigma was the "Prof's Book"

### Eccentricities During This Period
- Cycled to office wearing service gas mask during hay fever season
- Counted bicycle pedal revolutions to predict when chain would slip (rather than fixing it)
- Chained his mug to radiator pipes to prevent theft

### Language Style at Stage
- Highly practical and problem-focused; applied mathematical genius to concrete war problems
- Known for eccentricities but also for warm humor and collegiality with Hut 8 team

### Representative Works
- Functional specification of the Bombe
- Statistical cryptanalysis methods (Banburismus)
- "The Applications of Probability to Cryptography" and "Paper on Statistics of Repetitions" (classified until 2012)

## Stage 4: Post-War — Computing, Morphogenesis & Tragic End (1946–1954)
**Age:** 34–41 | **Years:** 1946–1954

### Key Events
- Joined National Physical Laboratory (NPL), London (1945); designed the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE)
- Frustrated by bureaucratic obstacles; plans for ACE were overshadowed by American projects
- Joined Max Newman's Computing Machine Laboratory at University of Manchester (1948)
- Contributed to development of Manchester computers (early software/programming)
- Wrote on neural networks and artificial intelligence
- Published "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (Mind, 1950) — introduced the Turing Test
- Elected Fellow of the Royal Society (FRS) (1951) for his 1936 achievement
- Published "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis" (1952) — mathematical biology, Turing patterns
- Arrested in February 1952 for homosexual acts; accepted hormone treatment (chemical castration) as alternative to prison
- Lost security clearance; disqualified from secret cryptological work
- Died June 7, 1954, at age 41, from cyanide poisoning (death ruled suicide)
- Inquest determined suicide, but evidence is also consistent with accidental poisoning

### Language Style at Stage
- More expansive and philosophical — as seen in "Computing Machinery and Intelligence"
- Defiant in his identity; intellectual individuality remained vibrant despite prosecution
- Some later writings show a growing interest in physics and fundamental questions

### Representative Works
- "Computing Machinery and Intelligence," Mind (1950)
- "The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis," Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B (1952)
- Unfinished work in biology and physics (1953–54)
