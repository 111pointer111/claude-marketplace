# EVENTS.md — Archimedes of Syracuse

## Timeline of Key Events

### Birth and Family (c. 287 BC)
- Born in Syracuse, Sicily (then part of Magna Graecia / "Great Greece")
- Father: Phidias, an astronomer (Archimedes' own statement in The Sand Reckoner)
- Family possibly of aristocratic origin; possibly related to King Hiero II

---

### Education in Alexandria (c. 270-260 BC)
- Studies with successors of Euclid in Alexandria, Egypt
- Befriends Conon of Samos (astronomer/mathematician) and Eratosthenes (future library director)
- Fully absorbs Alexandrian mathematics; develops lifelong correspondence habit

---

### Return to Syracuse and Service to Hiero II (c. 260-215 BC)
- Returns to Syracuse as a mature mathematician
- Serves King Hiero II as engineer and problem-solver
- Designs the Syracusia (largest ship of antiquity)
- Invents the Archimedes' screw for pumping water
- Possibly serves as a teacher/prince's tutor (dedicates The Sand Reckoner to Gelon, son of Hiero)

---

### The Golden Crown Problem (c. 240s BC)
- King Hiero suspects goldsmith of fraud in temple crown commission
- Requests Archimedes find a way to determine if gold was substituted without damaging crown
- Discovers buoyancy principle while bathing
- Runs naked through Syracuse shouting "Eureka! Eureka! I have found it!"
- Uses water displacement method to compare densities of gold, silver, and crown

---

### Mathematical Peak Period (c. 260-220 BC)
Completes most of his surviving mathematical treatises:

| Work | Subject |
|------|---------|
| On Plane Equilibriums I-II | Statics, center of gravity, lever law |
| Quadrature of the Parabola | Parabolic segment area = 4/3 inscribed triangle |
| On the Sphere and Cylinder | Sphere volume = 2/3 of circumscribing cylinder |
| Measurement of a Circle | π approximation between 310/71 and 317/71 |
| On Spirals | Archimedean spiral properties |
| On Conoids and Spheroids | Volumes of rotated conic sections |
| On Floating Bodies I-II | Hydrostatics, buoyancy principle |
| The Sand Reckoner | Large number system up to 8×10^63 |

---

### The Method of Mechanical Theorems (c. 250s BC)
- Writes to Eratosthenes describing his discovery method
- Uses physical/mechanical reasoning (lever law) to discover geometric theorems
- Insists these must be proven by rigorous geometric method afterward
- Work lost until 1906 discovery in Archimedes Palimpsest

---

### Siege of Syracuse (214-212 BC)
- Second Punic War: Syracuse switches from Rome to Carthage
- Roman General Marcellus besieges the city
- Archimedes' war machines deployed for defense:
  - Improved catapults and ballistas
  - "Archimedes' claw" — crane-like device to lift and destroy Roman ships
  - Possibly: heat ray (burning mirrors) — historically disputed
- Machines cause heavy Roman casualties; siege lasts ~2 years

---

### Death (212 or 211 BC)
Three versions (Plutarch):

**Version 1**: Absorbed in geometric diagram on the beach; Roman soldier orders him to come to Marcellus; refuses until he finishes his proof; soldier kills him.

**Version 2**: Soldier with drawn sword offers to kill him; Archimedes asks for a moment to finish his work; soldier ignores plea and kills him.

**Version 3**: Archimedes carrying mathematical instruments (dials, spheres, angles) to Marcellus; soldiers think he carries gold vessels; kill him.

**Marcellus' reaction**: Enraged; had ordered Archimedes not be harmed; called him a "geometrical Briareus" (hundred-armed giant).

---

### Tomb and Legacy

**Cicero discovers tomb (75 BC)**:
- Tomb overgrown with brambles in Syracuse
- Had sphere and cylinder carved on top per Archimedes' request
- Cicero restores and clears the site
- Inscription includes verses (half legible) referencing his most valued mathematical result

**Archimedes Palimpsest (1906)**:
- J.L. Heiberg discovers 10th-century manuscript in Constantinople
- Contains previously lost work The Method of Mechanical Theorems
- Sold at auction in 1998 for $2.2 million; now at Walters Art Museum, Baltimore

## Historical Context

- **Second Punic War** (218-201 BC) — background for siege
- **Hiero II's rule** (c. 270-215 BC) — Archimedes' primary patron
- **Roman conquest of Sicily** (212-211 BC) — ends with Archimedes' death
- **Cicero in Sicily** (75 BC) — discovers and restores tomb
