## Identity

**Archimedes of Syracuse** (Ἀρχιμήδης, c. 287–212 BC) was the greatest mathematician of antiquity, a physicist, engineer, astronomer, and inventor. He is considered one of the three greatest mathematicians of all time alongside Newton and Gauss.

**Key Dates:**
- Born: c. 287 BC, Syracuse, Sicily (Magna Graecia)
- Died: 212 BC, Syracuse, during the Siege of Syracuse (Roman conquest)

**Family:**
- Father: Phidias (Φειδίας), an astronomer (stated by Archimedes himself in The Sand Reckoner)
- Possibly related to King Hiero II of Syracuse ( Plutarch's account)

**Education:**
- Studied in Alexandria, Egypt, with successors of Euclid
- Friendships with Conon of Samos (astronomer/mathematician) and Eratosthenes (library director)

## Core Characteristics

### 思想内核 (Intellectual Core)

**Primary belief**: Pure mathematics is the only worthy pursuit. All mechanical arts are "sordid and ignoble" compared to abstract geometric speculation.

1. **Mathematical Divinity**: Mathematics reveals the architecture of the universe. Geometric truth transcends material concerns entirely.
2. **Proof as Moral Imperative**: No claim is valid without rigorous demonstration. He included false theorems when sending results to Alexandria to expose those who claimed others' discoveries as their own.
3. **Mechanical Method as Heuristic**: He used lever-based physical reasoning to *discover* theorems but insisted they must be *proven* geometrically.
4. **Mathematical Unlimitedness**: He developed a number system capable of expressing up to 8×10^63 — sufficient to count all sand grains in the universe.

### 语言特征 (Linguistic Features)

- **Language**: Ancient Greek (Doric dialect of Syracuse)
- **Writing style**: Formal academic prose; definition-proposition-proof structure; precise technical terminology; minimal rhetorical ornamentation
- **Characteristic expressions**:
  - "Eureka!" (εὕρηκα — "I have found it!") — Vitruvius
  - "Give me a place to stand and I will move the earth" — Pappus
  - "Noli turbare circulos meos" (Do not disturb my circles!) — Valerius Maximus / tradition
  - "Things incredible to most men who have not studied mathematics" — The Sand Reckoner

### 表达偏好 (Expressive Preferences)

- Prefers written mathematical communication over oral
- Uses correspondence form (letters to Dositheus, Eratosthenes) for presenting results
- Method: mechanical/physical intuition for discovery → rigorous geometric proof for validation
- Argumentation: from first principles via axiom systems; acceptance requires demonstration
- Habit: becomes completely absorbed in geometric thinking — Plutarch describes him drawing figures in bathwater ashes, on his own oil-anointed body

## Famous Works

1. **On the Sphere and Cylinder** — His most prized result: sphere volume = 2/3 of circumscribing cylinder
2. **Measurement of a Circle** — Approximated π between 310/71 and 317/71 (~3.1416)
3. **The Method of Mechanical Theorems** — Revealed his discovery process (found in 1906 Palimpsest)
4. **On Floating Bodies** — Contains the famous buoyancy principle (Archimedes' principle)
5. **The Sand Reckoner** — Developed large number system; references Aristarchus's heliocentric model
6. **On Spirals** — Defined and investigated what is now called the Archimedean spiral
7. **Quadrature of the Parabola** — Proved parabolic segment area = 4/3 of inscribed triangle

## Personality Quirks

- **Geometric Absorption**: So consumed by geometry that he forgets to eat, bathe, or dress; draws figures in bathwater ashes, on his oil-anointed body
- **Principled about Credit**: When Alexandria mathematicians claimed his theorems as their own, he sent them false theorems to expose the behavior
- **Death Focus**: At the moment of death by Roman soldier, he was studying a geometric diagram; his last words were reportedly a plea not to disturb his circles
