## 直接引语（原文/英文/中文）

### 1. "making objects out of fear"
- **来源**: Britannica — Rilke Biography
- **原文位置**: Development of new lyric poetry and New Poems 章节
- **原文上下文**: "In a letter to Lou Andreas-Salomé written in July 1903, he had defined his method with this formulation: 'making objects out of fear.'"
- **URL**: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rainer-Maria-Rilke

### 2. "Only a step, and my deepest misery could turn into bliss."
- **来源**: Britannica — Rilke Biography
- **原文位置**: The Notebook of Malte Laurids Brigge 章节
- **原文上下文**: "The work must be seen as the description of the disintegration of a soul—but a disintegration not devoid of a dialectic mental reservation: 'Only a step,' writes Malte, 'and my deepest misery could turn into bliss.'"
- **URL**: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rainer-Maria-Rilke

### 3. "God speaks to each of us as he makes us / then walks with us silently out of the night / These are the words we dimly hear: / You, sent out beyond your recall, / go to the limits of your longing. / Embody me."

- **原文位置**: The Book of Hours, Worpswede, and marriage 章节
- **URL**: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Rainer-Maria-Rilke

### 15. Britannica：杜伊诺哀歌创作过程
- **来源**: Britannica — Duino Elegies
- **原文**: "Rilke wrote the first two elegies, and began the third, while visiting the castle of Duino on the Adriatic in 1912; he finished the third in Paris in 1913 and completed the fourth and began the fifth in Munich in 1915. Emotionally paralyzed by World War I and its aftermath, Rilke wrote little more until February 1922, when in a burst of nearly manic productivity he revised the fifth poem, completed the remaining five of the cycle, and wrote the 55 poems that comprise the Sonnets to Orpheus, all in a span of three weeks."
- **URL**: https://www.britannica.com/topic/Duino-Elegies

### 16. Britannica：致奥尔弗斯的十四行诗主题
- **来源**: Britannica — Sonnets to Orpheus
- **原文**: "The Sonnets to Orpheus are concerned with the relationship of art and poetry to life. In them Rilke sought to show poetry's power to transmute problems of existence and to justify reality."
- **URL**: https://www.britannica.com/topic/Sonnets-to-Orpheus
